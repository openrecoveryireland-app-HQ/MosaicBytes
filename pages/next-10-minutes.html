Get Through the Next 10 Minutes

This page is for the moment when things feel too much and you need to slow everything down. You do not have to fix your whole life right now. You only have to get through the next ten minutes.

These steps are simple support steps. If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call emergency services on 999 or 112.

Pause and breathe

Stop for a moment. Before doing anything else, give yourself a chance to slow down. You do not have to answer every thought. You do not have to make a big decision right now. You only have to pause.

When something feels urgent, breathing can give you a small space between the feeling and the next action. That space can be enough to help you make a safer choice.

Put both feet on the floor if you can. Let your shoulders drop. Unclench your jaw. Try to breathe gently, not perfectly.

Simple steady breathing

Breathe in gently. Count slowly from 1 to 5 if that feels comfortable. Then breathe out gently, counting from 1 to 5 again. Repeat this for a few minutes.

The NHS describes this as a simple breathing exercise for stress.

NHS: Breathing exercises for stress

Belly breathing

Place one hand on your chest and one hand on your stomach. Breathe gently and notice the movement in your body. Try to let the breath move down towards your belly rather than staying high in your chest.

The HSE includes belly breathing as part of its stress-management exercises.

HSE: Belly breathing

Tension down breathing

If your body feels tight, tense, or wound up, try using your breathing to help your body soften. Sit or stand as comfortably as you can. Breathe gently. As you breathe out, allow your shoulders, jaw, hands, and stomach to loosen a little.

The HSE has a short “Tension down” breathing exercise for stress, anxiety, and low mood.

HSE: Exercises to manage stress

Box breathing

Box breathing is a four-part breathing pattern.

  • Breathe in for 4.
  • Hold for 4.
  • Breathe out for 4.
  • Hold for 4.

You can picture moving around the four sides of a square: in, hold, out, hold.

Cleveland Clinic: Box breathing

Tactical breathing

Tactical breathing is another steady breathing pattern used in high-pressure situations.

  • Breathe in while counting 1, 2, 3, 4.
  • Hold while counting 1, 2, 3, 4.
  • Breathe out while counting 1, 2, 3, 4.
  • Repeat for a few breaths.

A U.S. Navy medical document describes combat tactical breathing using a 4-count rhythm.

U.S. Navy: Combat tactical breathing PDF
The aim is not to do breathing perfectly. The aim is to slow the moment down. If holding your breath feels uncomfortable, skip the hold and just breathe in slowly and breathe out slowly. If focusing on your breathing makes you feel worse, stop and try another step.

Move away from the trigger

If something is pulling at you, move away from it if you can. That trigger might be a place, a person, a message, a memory, a shop, a pub, a bottle, a website, a thought, or an argument.

You do not have to stand there and fight it face to face. Distance helps.

  • Move to another room.
  • Step outside.
  • Put the phone down.
  • Leave the aisle.
  • Walk away from the door.
  • Close the laptop.
  • Change where your body is.

You are not being weak by moving away. You are protecting yourself. The aim is not to prove anything. The aim is to give yourself space.

Further explanation

SMART Recovery describes urge-management tools that include delaying, escaping, avoiding, distracting, and substituting addictive thinking.

SMART Recovery: DENTS urge-management tool
Try saying to yourself: “I do not have to deal with this from here.” Then move. One step away is still a step away.

Call someone safe

You do not have to handle this on your own. Call or message someone safe. This does not have to be a perfect person. It just needs to be someone who will not pressure you, shame you, wind you up, or make things worse.

You might contact a sponsor, a trusted friend, a family member, someone in recovery, a counsellor, a support worker, or someone who simply knows how to listen.

You do not need a big explanation. You can say:

  • “I’m having a hard ten minutes.”
  • “I need a steady voice.”
  • “Can you stay on the phone with me for a few minutes?”
  • “I do not need advice. I just need not to be on my own with this.”

If they do not answer, try someone else. Send one message. Make one call. Then make another if you need to.

Change your surroundings

Change something around you. When your head is racing, staying in the exact same place can keep the same loop going. You do not have to change your whole day. Just change your surroundings for a few minutes.

  • Stand up.
  • Move rooms.
  • Open a window.
  • Go outside.
  • Sit in a different chair.
  • Turn on a light.
  • Put on the kettle.
  • Step away from the bed, couch, door, phone, or wherever the feeling is getting stronger.

Small changes can interrupt the pattern. Your surroundings send signals to your brain. A different space can help create a different moment.

Grounding through your surroundings

The 5-4-3-2-1 grounding exercise uses the senses to bring attention back to the present moment. It can fit well with changing your surroundings because it asks you to notice what is around you.

University of Rochester Medical Center: 5-4-3-2-1 grounding
You are not trying to escape life. You are giving yourself a better chance to settle. Change the room. Change the air. Change the position of your body. Then take the next breath from there.

Do one small physical thing

Do one small thing with your body or your hands. Do not pick something big. Do not start a major job. Choose one tiny action that is easy to begin and easy to finish.

  • Make tea.
  • Wash a cup.
  • Splash your face.
  • Brush your teeth.
  • Put your shoes on.
  • Walk to the door.
  • Fold one towel.
  • Throw one piece of rubbish in the bin.
  • Fill a glass of water.

The point is not the task itself. The point is to get out of your head and back into the present moment.

Using activity to ride out an urge

SMART Recovery includes distraction and substitution as part of its urge-management tools. A small physical task can give your mind and body something safe and real to follow.

SMART Recovery: DENTS urge-management tool
Keep it tiny. One action. One movement. One thing you can finish. When it is done, remind yourself: “I did one safe thing.”

Wait ten minutes

You do not have to solve everything now. You only have to wait ten minutes.

Ten minutes is not forever. It is not a lifetime. It is just a short space of time where you agree not to act on the feeling.

  • Set a timer if that helps.
  • Do not argue with every thought.
  • Do not try to win the whole battle in your head.
  • Just delay the next action.

You can breathe. You can move away. You can call someone. You can change rooms. You can do one small physical thing. You can sit and let the feeling pass through without obeying it.

Urges can rise and fall

Urge surfing is a technique for noticing an urge without acting on it. Dartmouth Health explains that urges often pass if they are not fed by rumination, planning, or giving them attention.
